## How to Use This Toolkit

This toolkit is not a book you read once. It is a reference system you return to.

**Start here:** Read the six reset routines. Identify which two feel most relevant to your current life. Practice those until they feel familiar. Then expand to the emotional regulation exercises.

**Use the worksheets** to track patterns over 2–4 weeks. You will begin to see your stress signature — the triggers, the physical signs, the times of day — and your regulation map will become personalised.

**Print the reference cards.** Put them where life happens: your desk, your fridge, your car dashboard, your bathroom mirror. Regulation at its most effective is regulation that doesn't require effort to access.

## Part One: Step-by-Step Reset Routines

### Routine 1: Morning Wake-Up Reset (5 minutes)

*Use this before reaching for your phone. It sets your nervous system's baseline for the day.*

**Step 1 — Orient (60 seconds)**
Without moving from where you are, slowly look around the room. Let your eyes travel to three things you find pleasant, neutral, or interesting. Name them internally. Take one slow breath.

**Step 2 — Shake and Wake (60 seconds)**
Sit on the edge of the bed. Shake your hands and wrists loosely. Roll your shoulders backward three times. Nod your head slowly yes, then no. Gently tilt each ear toward the shoulder.

**Step 3 — Breathe Down (90 seconds)**
Inhale through your nose for 4 counts, filling your belly first, then your chest.
Hold at the top for 2 counts.
Exhale through your mouth for 6 counts — long and complete.
Repeat 4 times.

**Step 4 — Body Scan (60 seconds)**
Starting at the top of your head, move attention slowly down through your body. Notice tension without trying to fix it. When you find a tight spot, breathe into it on the inhale, and imagine releasing it on the exhale.

**Step 5 — Intention (30 seconds)**
Before standing, say one sentence to yourself: *Today I am choosing [one quality — patience, focus, presence, ease].* This is not a goal. It is an orientation.

---

### Routine 2: Midday Crash Reset (5 minutes)

*Use this at the 2–3pm energy dip, after a difficult meeting, or when brain fog rolls in.*

**Step 1 — Cold Water Activation (60 seconds)**
Run cold water over the inside of your wrists for 30 seconds. Splash a small amount on the back of your neck. Pat dry.

This triggers the mammalian dive reflex — a hard-wired physiological response that slows heart rate, increases focus, and gives the nervous system an immediate reset signal.

**Step 2 — Stand and Stretch (60 seconds)**
Stand up. Reach both arms overhead and interlace your fingers, palms facing the ceiling. Stretch for 5 counts. Release. Shake arms out. Roll ankles slowly one direction, then the other.

**Step 3 — Box Breathing (2 minutes)**
Inhale for 4 counts. Hold for 4 counts. Exhale for 4 counts. Hold for 4 counts.
Repeat 4–5 rounds.

Box breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system and improves cognitive clarity. Used by military special forces before high-stakes operations.

**Step 4 — Sensory Reset (60 seconds)**
Drink a glass of water slowly. Or eat one piece of fruit with full attention. Or step outside and feel the air on your skin for 60 seconds.

Slow, intentional sensory experience signals to your nervous system: *I am not in danger. I have enough. I am here.*

### Routine 3: Emotional Spike Reset (5 minutes)

*Use this when you feel anger rising, tears behind your eyes, or panic beginning. Use it before a difficult conversation, not instead of it.*

**Step 1 — Name It (30 seconds)**
Say out loud or in writing: "I am feeling [anger / anxiety / overwhelm / grief]. This is a nervous system response. It will pass."

Naming an emotional state reduces amygdala activation by up to 50% (UCLA research, Lieberman et al., 2007). This is not dismissal — it is regulation.

**Step 2 — Physiological Sigh (60 seconds)**
Take a double inhale through your nose (one normal breath, then a quick additional sniff at the top). Then a long, complete exhale through your mouth.

Repeat 3–4 times.

The physiological sigh is the fastest known method for reducing acute stress — it collapses the alveoli in your lungs that balloon during emotional spikes and rapidly lowers CO2 levels.

**Step 3 — Movement Discharge (90 seconds)**
Walk briskly around the room. Do 10 jumping jacks. Shake your whole body out for 30 seconds. Or press your palms hard against a wall for 10 counts.

Emotional activation is energy mobilised for action. Movement lets it discharge through the body rather than turning inward.

**Step 4 — Ground and Breathe (90 seconds)**
Sit down. Both feet flat on the floor. Both palms on your thighs. Press down with intention.
Take 5 slow belly breaths. Let each exhale be audible.

**Step 5 — Soften (30 seconds)**
Unclench your jaw. Drop your shoulders. Soften the skin around your eyes. Rest your tongue gently at the roof of your mouth.

These micro-releases signal muscular safety to your nervous system.

### Routine 4: Pre-Sleep Wind-Down Reset (7 minutes)

*Use this within 30 minutes of your intended sleep time. Replace screen time with this.*

**Step 1 — Environment (2 minutes)**
Dim all lights. If possible, use warm candlelight or a salt lamp. Lower the thermostat or open a window slightly. Put your phone in another room or face-down across the room.

Your environment sends your nervous system cues before you consciously begin to wind down. Light and temperature are powerful regulatory inputs.

**Step 2 — Progressive Muscle Relaxation (3 minutes)**
Lying down, tense and release each muscle group from feet to face:
- Feet and calves — squeeze 5 counts, release
- Thighs — squeeze 5 counts, release
- Glutes — squeeze 5 counts, release
- Belly — pull in 5 counts, release
- Fists — squeeze 5 counts, spread fingers wide, release
- Shoulders — shrug to ears 5 counts, drop
- Jaw — clench 3 counts, release and let hang open

After each release, breathe out audibly and feel the muscle soften.

**Step 3 — Extended Exhale Breathing (2 minutes)**
Breathe in for 4. Out for 8.
Repeat until the timer ends or you drift.

The 4:8 ratio is particularly effective for sleep onset — the long exhale activates the vagal brake and slows your heart rate to sleep-ready levels.

### Routine 5: ADHD Freeze Reset (5 minutes)

*Use this when your brain has hit the wall — frozen, can't start, everything feels impossible.*

**Step 1 — Name the State (30 seconds)**
Say it out loud: "I am in freeze. This is dopamine depletion, not laziness. My brain needs a different signal."

ADHD freeze is neurological, not motivational. Treating it with shame or force deepens it. Treating it with understanding and movement opens it.

**Step 2 — Physical Activation (2 minutes)**
Choose one:
- Walk briskly for 2 minutes (even just up and down the hall)
- Do 15 jumping jacks
- Put on one energising song and move to it however feels natural
- Do 10 slow pushups against the wall

Movement releases dopamine. Dopamine breaks freeze. This is not optional.

**Step 3 — Reduce the Field (60 seconds)**
Close all browser tabs except one. Silence your phone. If you're in a noisy environment, put on headphones with brown noise or lo-fi music. If possible, move to a quieter location.

ADHD freeze is often compounded by sensory overload. Reducing stimulation lets the prefrontal cortex come back online.

**Step 4 — The One Tiny Thing (90 seconds)**
Ask yourself: *What is the smallest possible action I could take in the next 2 minutes?*
Not the project. Not the goal. One tiny action.
Open the document. Send one email. Write one sentence. Wash three dishes.

Do it now. Immediately. Before your brain offers reasons not to.

Dopamine is released on task completion, however small. That release creates the neurochemical momentum for the next action. Start tiny. Let the system build.

### Routine 6: Post-Conflict Recovery Reset (5 minutes)

*Use this after a difficult conversation, an argument with your partner, a hard moment with your child, or a tense work interaction.*

**Step 1 — Discharge (60 seconds)**
If you're alone, allow yourself a genuine emotional expression: cry if you need to, make an angry sound into a pillow, say what you wish you'd said (privately). Do not perform calm before you've actually felt what you feel.

**Step 2 — Physiological Sigh x3 (30 seconds)**
Double inhale through the nose. Long exhale through the mouth. Repeat 3 times.

**Step 3 — Cold Water (60 seconds)**
Splash cold water on your face or wrists. This activates the dive reflex and interrupts the physiological arousal cycle that conflict creates.

**Step 4 — Compassion Breath (2 minutes)**
Sit quietly. As you breathe in, say internally: *I am doing my best.*
As you breathe out, say: *So are they.*
Repeat for 2 minutes.

This is not about excusing harmful behaviour. It is about releasing the cortisol spike that keeps your nervous system locked in re-enactment long after the conflict has ended.

**Step 5 — Anchor (60 seconds)**
Name three things that are stable, good, or true in your life right now. Say them quietly. Feel the steadiness of what holds.

## Part Two: Emotional Regulation Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Anger Audit (5 minutes)

**The signal:** You feel anger, frustration, or rage — at a person, a situation, yourself, or nothing specific.

**Body work first:**
Before engaging with the content of the anger, do the movement discharge from Routine 3, Step 3. 2 full minutes of movement. Then return here.

**The audit (3 minutes):**
Ask yourself three questions:
1. *What did I actually need in that moment that I didn't get?*
2. *Is there a fear underneath this anger?*
3. *What would I want someone to understand about how I feel right now?*

Write the answers down if possible. Writing externalises the loop so your brain stops running it internally.

**ADHD adaptation:** Use voice memo instead of writing. Say the answers out loud.

**The release:** After the audit, take one long physiological sigh. Let it go — not the situation, just the cortisol. You can address the situation from a regulated place.

---

### Exercise 2: Anxiety Interrupt (5 minutes)

**The signal:** Racing thoughts, "what if" loops, physical tension, heart racing with no immediate cause.

**Step 1 — Reality-test the threat:**
On a scale of 1–10, how likely is the feared outcome to actually happen?
If it did happen — what would you actually do?
Have you survived difficult things before?

**Step 2 — 4-7-8 Breathing:**
Inhale for 4 counts. Hold for 7. Exhale for 8.
Repeat 4 times.

The extended hold increases oxygen exchange and the long exhale activates the vagal brake. This pattern is specifically calibrated to interrupt the physiological anxiety loop.

**Step 3 — The 5-4-3-2-1 Ground:**
5 things you see. 4 you can touch. 3 you hear. 2 you smell. 1 you taste.
This floods the sensory cortex with present data, interrupting the anxiety's grip on the default mode network.

**ADHD adaptation:** Walk while doing the 5-4-3-2-1. Name things out loud. Use your phone's voice recorder if needed.

---

### Exercise 3: Overwhelm Triage (5 minutes)

**The signal:** Everything feels urgent and impossible simultaneously. Paralysis. The feeling that you're failing everything and everyone.

**Step 1 — Externalise:**
Take a blank page or sticky notes. Write every task, worry, and demand that is swirling in your head. All of it. Do not sort or prioritize — just empty.

**Step 2 — Body reset first:**
Do the 3-breath reset from Chapter 4 before you do anything else with the list.

**Step 3 — Sort into 3 piles:**
- *True urgent* (needs action today, concrete consequence if skipped)
- *Feels urgent* (emotionally weighty but not time-critical)
- *Later* (would be good to do, no real deadline)

Most overwhelm lives in the "feels urgent" pile. Seeing it there reduces its grip.

**Step 4 — One thing:**
From the "true urgent" pile, identify the single most important next action. Just one. Write it on a fresh piece of paper. Put everything else away. Begin.

---

### Exercise 4: Shame Release (10 minutes)

*This exercise is for after you've snapped at your kids, failed to show up as you wanted to, or are carrying guilt about not being "enough."*

**Step 1 — Self-compassion first:**
Place your hand on your heart. Take a breath. Say: "This is a moment of suffering. Suffering is part of being human. May I be kind to myself in this moment."

This is Dr. Kristin Neff's self-compassion practice. Research shows it reduces cortisol and increases resilience more effectively than self-criticism or positive affirmation.

**Step 2 — Distinguish shame from guilt:**
Guilt says: *I did something wrong.*
Shame says: *I am wrong.*

Guilt is useful — it motivates repair. Shame is dysregulating — it keeps you stuck.

Ask: *Is this guilt or shame?*

**Step 3 — Repair or release:**
If guilt: identify one concrete action that constitutes repair. Do it as soon as reasonable.
If shame: write or say, "I am not my worst moments. I am a person who is trying."

**Step 4 — Breathe:**
Four slow breath cycles. Let each exhale be longer than each inhale.

---

### Exercise 5: Grief Tending (10 minutes)

*For loss of any kind: a person, a version of your life, an expectation, a relationship, a season of childhood you're watching pass.*

**Step 1 — Name what you're grieving.**
Be specific. Write it if you can.

**Step 2 — Allow the body:**
Sit quietly and allow whatever physical sensation wants to arise. Do not perform grief or suppress it. If tears come, let them. If numbness, allow it. If anger, let it be.

Your body knows how to grieve if you let it.

**Step 3 — Hold it with warmth:**
Place both hands on your chest. Breathe slowly. Say: *What I feel is real. What I lost mattered. I am allowed to grieve.*

**Step 4 — Return:**
After 5–10 minutes, gently orient to the room. Name three things you can see. Take one full breath. Thank yourself for making space.

---

### Exercise 6: Numbness Awakening (5 minutes)

*For when you feel nothing — disconnected, flat, going through motions.*

Numbness is a protective nervous system response. It is freeze energy. The answer is gentle, non-threatening activation.

**Step 1 — Temperature contrast:**
Hold ice cubes for 30 seconds. Then run warm water over your hands for 30 seconds. Repeat twice.

**Step 2 — Humming:**
Hum any note with your lips closed for 60 seconds. Feel the vibration in your chest, throat, and skull. This stimulates the vagus nerve and gently activates the system.

**Step 3 — Noticing game:**
Name 10 things in your environment that you find even slightly interesting, pleasant, or curious. Not beautiful or meaningful — just mildly interesting.

**Step 4 — Gentle movement:**
Walk slowly for 2 minutes. Notice your feet touching the ground with each step. Notice the sensation of the air. Let curiosity, however small, lead.
